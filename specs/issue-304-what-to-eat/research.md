## PMC citations — verified valid for this article

### Tier 1: Direct alcohol-food pharmacokinetics (load-bearing for our claims)

1. **Jones AW, Jönsson KA, Kechagias S. 1997. Effect of high-fat, high-protein, and high-carbohydrate meals on the pharmacokinetics of a small dose of ethanol.** *Br J Clin Pharmacol* 44(6):521-6. PubMed: 9431825.
   - Key finding: Peak BAC: fat 16.6, carb 17.7, protein 13.3 mg/dl vs fasting 30.8 mg/dl. **All three macros roughly equivalent — gastric emptying is the mechanism, not specific macro identity.**

2. **Mitchell MC Jr, Teigen EL, Ramchandani VA. 2014. Absorption and Peak Blood Alcohol Concentration After Drinking Beer, Wine, or Spirits.** *Alcohol Clin Exp Res*. PMC4112772.
   - Key finding: Fasting peak BAC: spirits 77.4, wine 61.7, beer 50.3 mg/dl. Spirits peak fastest (36 min) vs beer (62 min). Higher concentration → faster absorption.

3. **Holt S. 1981. Observations on the relation between alcohol absorption and the rate of gastric emptying.** *Can Med Assoc J* 124(3):267-77. PMC1705129. PubMed: 7459787.
   - Foundational mechanism: gastric emptying rate is the dominant variable controlling alcohol absorption.

4. **Fisher JM, Wolever TMS, Campbell JE, Ezatagha A, Noronha JC, Jenkins AL. 2020. Effect of a Snack Bar Optimized to Reduce Alcohol Bioavailability: A Randomized Controlled Clinical Trial in Healthy Individuals.** *J Med Food* 23(4). PMC7185312.
   - 50.4% reduction in peak BAC vs fasting. Best caloric efficiency: 24% per 100 cal. Macronutrient: 20g protein, 9g fat, 30g carb, 5g insoluble fiber. **This is the load-bearing modern human RCT.**

### Tier 2: Mechanism / supporting

5. **Butts M, Sundaram VL, Murughiyan U, Borthakur A, Singh S. 2023. The Influence of Alcohol Consumption on Intestinal Nutrient Absorption: A Comprehensive Review.** *Nutrients* 15(7):1571. PMC10096942.
   - Alcohol impairs absorption of B1/thiamine, folate, magnesium, zinc, calcium, glutamine, glycine. Justifies eating nutrient-dense foods *before* alcohol exposure rather than relying on after.

6. **Wiese J, McPherson S, Odden MC, Shlipak MG. 2004. Effect of Opuntia ficus indica on symptoms of the alcohol hangover.** *Arch Intern Med* 164(12):1334-40. PubMed: 15226168.
   - 64-subject RCT. Pre-loading with prickly pear extract 5 hrs before drinking reduced nausea, dry mouth, anorexia by anti-inflammatory mechanism.

### Tier 3: Glutathione / cysteine / antioxidant pathway (food-relevant)

7. **Choi J, et al. 2023. Combination of Cysteine and Glutathione Prevents Ethanol-Induced Hangover and Liver Damage by Modulation of Nrf2 Signaling.** *Antioxidants* (MDPI). PMC10604027.
   - 3:1 cysteine:glutathione ratio improved alcohol metabolism, lowered serum acetaldehyde. Justifies eating high-cysteine foods (eggs, dairy, alliums) pre-drink.

8. **Eriksson CJP, et al. 2020. L-cysteine containing tablets reduce hangover symptoms — RCT.** *Alcohol Alcohol*. PubMed: 32808029.
   - Pre-loading cysteine reduced nausea, headache, anxiety in human RCT.

### Tier 4: Specific food / nutrient mechanisms

9. **Hayes DJ, et al. 2024. GABAergic signaling in alcohol use disorder and withdrawal.** *Pharmacol Ther*. PMC10623140. (Connects pre-drink prep to GABA rebound — bridges to hangxiety guide.)

10. **Boyle NB, et al. 2024. Magnesium for anxiety, sleep, stress.** *Nutrients*. PMC11136869. (Magnesium-rich foods: leafy greens, pumpkin seeds, salmon — pre-drink rationale.)

11. **He YX, et al. 2024. Hovenia dulcis: alcohol-associated liver disease review.** *Front Pharmacol*. PMC11033337. (Connects to DHM/Hovenia stack on top of food prep.)

12. **Choi JS, et al. 2024. Hovenia dulcis extract human RCT (n=25).** *Foods* 13(24):4021. PMC11675335. (Pre-drink supplementation evidence.)
